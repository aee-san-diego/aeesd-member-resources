You can learn about all of the valuable certifications that [[Association of Energy Engineers|AEE]] offers on the national website:
https://www.aeecenter.org/aee-certifications/

However, here are few that we would like to highlight as particularly valuable in the San Diego region:

---
# Certified Energy Auditor (CEA)

For junior and mid-career engineers—particularly those coming from more academic backgrounds, product manufacturing, or new construction design—the Certified Energy Auditor (CEA) credential serves as a helpful bridge to the world of existing buildings. While design engineering often lives in models and spreadsheets, [[Energy Audit|auditing]] requires a physical presence in the field; it is about understanding how buildings actually operate, not just how they were designed to work. In the San Diego market, this distinction is real. With numerous California utility programs requiring specific auditing standards and a robust ecosystem of Energy Service Companies (ESCOs) driving retrofit projects, the CEA validates your ability to assess existing facilities accurately. It signals to employers that you are not just capable of doing the math, but are also ready to walk the site, identify practical opportunities, and navigate the complexities of existing building systems.

https://www.aeecenter.org/certified-energy-auditor/

---
# Certified Building Commissioning Professionals (CBCP)

Suggested for mid-career and senior engineers, the Certified Building Commissioning Professional (CBCP) is a credential for those ready to operate at the top tier of the technical ladder. [[Commissioning]] is a rigorous discipline that goes beyond design; it requires an expert-level grasp of building systems, often demanding that the engineer understands the equipment even better than the installers to verify performance and catch errors. This role also introduces a critical layer of "soft skills" involving [[Contracts|contract]] interpretation and conflict management, as commissioning agents frequently navigate the friction between building owners and contractors. Because this position requires such a rare blend of deep technical acuity, contractual literacy, and field confidence, commissioning professionals often command some of the highest salaries on the technical track. For engineers in San Diego seeking to maximize their income while remaining hands-on with complex systems, the CBCP is a powerful differentiator that opens doors to lucrative, dynamic roles that often include travel and high-profile project work.

https://www.aeecenter.org/certified-building-commissioning-professional/

# Solar PV Professional (SPVP)

The Certified Solar PV Professional (SPVP) is an excellent credential for recent graduates or experienced engineers—particularly those with Mechanical or Industrial engineering backgrounds—who want to expand their versatility. While traditional energy engineering often focuses on the demand side (HVAC, lighting, controls), San Diego’s status as having one of the highest densities of photovoltaic installations in the nation means that supply-side knowledge is in higher demand. For professionals who do not have a formal Electrical Engineering degree, this certification validates a foundational understanding of PV technologies, design principles, and economics. In a region where solar is common, adding the SPVP to your resume bridges the gap between mechanical systems and renewable generation, helping you stand out as a well-rounded engineer capable of seeing the "whole building" energy picture.

https://www.aeecenter.org/aee-certifications/solar-pv-professional/


---
# Certified Measurement & Verification Professional (CMVP)

The Certified Measurement and Verification Professional (CMVP) is a specialized credential primarily suited for mid-career and senior engineers, though it is also a powerful accelerant for junior engineers specifically hired into M&V roles. [[Measurement and Verification]] is a discipline defined by "tacit knowledge"—skills and methodologies that are almost never taught in university engineering programs and must be learned on the job. Unlike the operational, hands-on focus of commissioning, M&V requires a rigorous analytical skillset focused on data crunching, statistical analysis, and baseline modeling. In San Diego, this expertise is essential for two major industry drivers: verifying savings for California utility programs and determining financial payouts for ESCO performance contracts. Because this role sits at the intersection of deep technical analysis and strict contractual compliance, the CMVP signals that you possess the rare ability to mathematically prove the value of energy projects and manage the financial stakes of long-term guarantees.

https://www.aeecenter.org/certified-measurement-verification-professional/
